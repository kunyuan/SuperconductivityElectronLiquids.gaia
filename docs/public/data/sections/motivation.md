# Introduction: Motivation and Background

This section poses the central question of the paper: can the Coulomb pseudopotential $\mu^*$ be computed from first principles, and can this yield quantitative predictions of $T_c$ for simple metals? The reasoning begins by establishing the BCS theory of superconductivity and the adiabatic approximation ($\omega_D/E_F \ll 1$, belief 0.90), from which the Migdal-Eliashberg framework (belief 0.95) is derived as a controlled low-energy effective theory. The section then catalogs the failures of the phenomenological approach: using the McMillan formula with $\mu^* = 0.1$ overestimates $T_c$ of aluminum by 58%, zinc by 57%, and lithium by three orders of magnitude. These discrepancies motivate the need for a microscopic treatment of $\mu^*$. The section also introduces the BTS renormalization relation (belief 0.99), which connects $\mu^*$ across energy scales, and notes that the RPA predicts an unphysical attractive $\mu^*$ for $r_s \gtrsim 2$ --- a prediction that will be contradicted by the vDiagMC results in Section IV. Belief propagation confirms that the experimental $T_c$ values remain highly credible (belief $\geq 0.99$ for Al and Zn, 1.00 for Li), while the phenomenological predictions are substantially downweighted (belief 0.40 for Al and Zn, 0.13 for Li), reflecting their poor explanatory power.
