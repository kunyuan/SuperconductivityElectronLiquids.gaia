# Conventional Superconductors

This section assembles the full ab initio $T_c$ prediction workflow and confronts it with experiment across five simple metals. The workflow (belief 0.99) combines three components --- the downfolded BSE, the vDiagMC-derived $\mu^*$, and DFPT-computed $\lambda$ --- through a composite strategy that first establishes $\mu^*$ availability for simple metals (though this intermediate claim has low belief at 0.40 due to propagation from the vDiagMC uncertainty) and then deduces the complete parameter-free pipeline. For aluminum ($T_c^{\mathrm{th}} = 1.1 \pm 0.3$ K vs. experiment 1.2 K, belief 0.93) and zinc ($T_c^{\mathrm{th}} = 0.7 \pm 0.3$ K vs. experiment 0.875 K, belief 0.93), the abduction against experimental data strongly favors the ab initio prediction over the phenomenological alternative, which overestimates both by ~57%. The most dramatic success is lithium: the ab initio prediction of $T_c \lesssim 10^{-3}$ K (belief 0.96) matches experiment ($4 \times 10^{-4}$ K), while the phenomenological prediction overshoots by three orders of magnitude and collapses to belief 0.13. The framework also predicts non-monotonic pressure dependence for aluminum's $T_c$ (belief 0.79) and identifies sodium and magnesium as lying near the quantum phase transition boundary (belief 0.79), where $\mu^*$ nearly or fully cancels $\lambda$, making superconductivity exponentially fragile.
